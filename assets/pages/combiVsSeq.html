Combinational Circuits

What are Combinational Circuits?

Combinational Circuits are digital circuits that provide an output instantaneously based on the current input. Specifically, they listen for input signals and produce an output without regard for the previous state of the inputs or outputs. They are designed using multiple logic gates in a way that creates a lack of feedback or memory components.

Classification of Combinational Circuits

Combinational Circuits can generally be classified into three different categories based on the functions they exhibit. This includes the following:

Arithmetic and Logical Functions

These circuits are capable of handling mathematical and logical operations, such as addition, subtraction, and comparison of binary numbers.

Data Transmission

These circuits manage data flow within a system by directing and controlling data transfer between components.

Code Converters

These circuits are responsible for converting information from one code or number system to another, such as converting binary values to decimal or other formats.

Sequential Circuits

What are Sequential Circuits?

Sequential circuits are digital circuits that store and use previous state information to determine their next state. This means that it uses both current input and previous output variables to determine the next output. It accomplishes this with the help of memory elements capable of storing binary information, such as latches or flip-flops. They are also created using logic gates.

Classification of Sequential Circuits

Sequential Circuits can be classified into two categories based on how they get their clock signal. This includes the following:

Asynchronous Sequential Circuits

Asynchronous Sequential Circuits do not have a global clock signal. Instead, they use the input pulses. Specifically, the output’s state immediately changes as a result of a clock pulse and a change in the input signal. These circuits are faster than their synchronous counterpart. For that reason, they are utilized when the speed of operation is emphasized.

Synchronous Sequential Circuits

Synchronous Sequential Circuits utilize a global clock signal to synchronize state transitions. The inputs can either be level-triggered or pulsed. In a clocked system, the pulse typically aligns with the clock pulse. Because operations are reliant on the clock’s frequency, they are generally slower than their asynchronous counterpart. They have to wait for the next active clock edge. However, the output state remains locked until triggered by the next clock or input pulse.

Clock Signal and Triggering

Clock Signal

In the previous sections, it was mentioned that Sequential Circuits are reliant on a Clock Signal. A Clock Signal is a type of control signal that allows for the elements of synchronous circuits to be in phase. It is derived from a square wave that has clearly defined high and low levels.

Triggering

The change of state in a circuit occurs at a specific point during the square wave. Triggering is the term used to refer to this specific point or time. It can be categorized into two types:

Level Triggering

In Level Triggering, the change of state occurs when a circuit is at a specific state, either high or low. Since the clock signal can take two possible states, there are also two types of Level Triggering. Positive Level Triggering is defined with the change in state occurring at the high time in the clock cycle, while Negative Level Triggering is referred to when the change in state is in the low state of the clock signal.

Edge Triggering

Edge Triggering occurs when the change in state occurs during a transition of the clock signal. A transition, in this case, is a change from a low to a high state, or vice versa, of the clock signal. There are also two kinds of Edge Triggering. Positive Edge Triggering occurs at the rising edge, when the clock signal transitions from low to high. In contrast, Negative Edge Triggering is the opposite, in which the change occurs on the falling edge, or the high-to-low transition.
